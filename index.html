END HUNGER, ACHIEVE FOOD SECURITY AND IMPROVED NUTRITION AND PROMOTE SUSTAINABLE AGRICULTURE.
Hunger is the leading cause of death in the world. Our planet has provided us with tremendous resources, but unequal access and inefficient handling leave millions of people malnourished. Despite progress in reducing undernourishment through economic growth and agricultural advancements, hunger and malnutrition remain critical global issues, exacerbated by environmental degradation, climate change, conflict, and inequality. As of 2022, 735 million people faced chronic hunger, and 2.4 billion experienced moderate to severe food insecurity. The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) aim to end all forms of hunger and malnutrition by 2030 through sustainable agricultural practices, supporting small-scale farmers, improving access to nutritious food, and fostering international cooperation. Achieving this goal will require collective action to ensure food security for all.

Goal 2 aims to end hunger, achieve food security, and promote sustainable agriculture. Goal 2 of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) aims to eliminate hunger, achieve food security, and promote sustainable agriculture by 2030. Despite significant progress in reducing undernourishment, millions still suffer from chronic hunger and malnutrition, driven by environmental degradation, climate change, conflict, and inequality. The global food crisis has worsened in recent years, with 735 million people experiencing chronic hunger and 2.4 billion facing food insecurity as of 2022. Achieving this goal requires a multi-faceted approach, including supporting small-scale farmers, ensuring equitable access to resources, investing in agricultural technology, and promoting sustainable food production systems. International cooperation is crucial in addressing food insecurity and malnutrition, particularly in vulnerable regions. Organizations like UNICEF, WHO, and the World Bank play key roles in monitoring progress and implementing initiatives to combat hunger. Ending hunger is essential for sustainable development, as malnutrition affects health, productivity, and economic stability. A collective global effort is required to create resilient food systems, improve nutrition, and ensure that everyone has access to safe and nutritious food year-round.

How many people are hungry?
It is projected that more than 600 million people worldwide will be facing hunger in 2030, highlighting the immense challenge of achieving the zero hunger target. People experiencing moderate food insecurity are typically unable to eat a healthy, balanced diet on a regular basis because of income or other resource constraints.

Why are there so many hungry people?
Shockingly, the world is back at hunger levels not seen since 2005, and food prices remain higher in more countries than in the period 2015–2019. Along with conflict, climate shocks, and rising cost of living, civil insecurity and declining food production have all contributed to food scarcity and high food prices. Investment in the agriculture sector is critical for reducing hunger and poverty, improving food security, creating employment and building resilience to disasters and shocks.

Why should I care?
We all want our families to have enough food to eat what is safe and nutritious. A world with zero hunger can positively impact our economies, health, education, equality and social development. It’s a key piece of building a better future for everyone. Additionally, with hunger limiting human development, we will not be able to achieve the other sustainable development goals such as education, health and gender equality.

How can we achieve Zero Hunger?
Food security requires a multi-dimensional approach – from social protection to safeguard safe and nutritious food especially for children to transforming food systems to achieve a more inclusive and sustainable world. There will need to be investments in rural and urban areas and in social protection so poor people have access to food and can improve their livelihoods.

What can we do to help?
You can make changes in your own life—at home, at work and in the community—by supporting local farmers or markets and making sustainable food choices, supporting good nutrition for all, and fighting food waste. You can also use your power as a consumer and voter, demanding businesses and governments make the choices and changes that will make Zero Hunger a reality. Join the conversation, whether on social media platforms or in your local communities.

Indian Government Schemes towards ZERO HUNGER


POSHAN Abhiyaan (National Nutrition Mission)

Launched: March 2018 by Prime Minister Narendra Modi in Jhunjhunu, Rajasthan.
Ministry: Ministry of Women and Child Development (MWCD).
Objective: To reduce malnutrition and achieve a malnutrition-free India through convergence of various nutrition-related schemes.
Target Groups:

  • Children (0-6 years)
  • Adolescent girls
  • Pregnant women
  • Lactating mothers

    • Goals & Targets: Reduce stunting, under-nutrition, anemia, and low birth weight by: 2% per annum (stunting, under-nutrition, low birth weight) 3% per annum (anemia in young children, women, and adolescent girls) Target: Reduce stunting from 38.4% (NFHS-4) to 25% by 2022 (Mission 25 by 2022). Improve nutritional status in a time-bound manner.

    Key Features & Strategies:
  • Multi-ministerial & Convergence Approach: Integrates schemes directly/indirectly affecting nutrition (e.g., ICDS, Mid-Day Meal, NHM). Focus on synergy and coordination to improve delivery.
  • Nutritional Strategies & Interventions: IYCF (Infant & Young Child Feeding), Food & Nutrition (diet diversity, food fortification), Immunization, Institutional Delivery, WASH (Water, Sanitation & Hygiene), De-worming, ORS-Zinc, Dietary Diversification, Adolescent Nutrition, Maternal Health & Nutrition, Early Childhood Development (ECD)/Early Childhood Care & Education (ECCE), ICT-RTM (Real-Time Monitoring via Poshan Tracker), Capacity Building & Training of Anganwadi Workers.
  • Technology Integration & Monitoring:
    • Poshan Tracker: ICT-enabled platform for real-time monitoring of supplementary nutrition delivery.
    • Growth Monitoring Devices (GMDs): Infantometer, Stadiometer, Weighing Scale for Mother & Infant at Anganwadi Centres. Sectoral Evaluation by DMEO, NITI Aayog found satisfactory performance in relevance, sustainability, and equity.
    • Integration with Mission Saksham Anganwadi & Poshan 2.0 (2021-22): Focus on diet diversity, food fortification, traditional knowledge, and millets. Use of millets in Hot Cooked Meals & Take Home Rations at Anganwadi Centres. Only fortified rice is supplied to States/UTs. Strengthened nutritional quality, testing, and delivery mechanisms.

    • Antyodaya Anna Yojana (AAY)

    Launch Date: 25th December 2000
    Ministry: Ministry of Consumer Affairs, Food & Public Distribution, Government of India
    Developed By: N. Sri Vishnu, former Union Food and Civil Supplies Minister
    First Implemented In: Rajasthan
    Objective:

  • To ensure food security for the poorest of the poor.
  • To provide subsidized food grains and essential commodities.
  • To reduce hunger and malnutrition in India.
  • To strengthen the Targeted Public Distribution System (TPDS).

    • Target Groups (Beneficiaries):
  • Rural Beneficiaries: Families with an annual income below Rs. 15,000, Small & marginal farmers, Landless agricultural laborers, Physically handicapped persons, Old-age pensioners (60+ years), Destitute widows, Rural artisans/craftsmen (potters, weavers, blacksmiths, carpenters), Tribal families in remote areas.
  • Urban Beneficiaries: Slum dwellers. Daily wage laborers (rickshaw pullers, porters, rag pickers, cobblers), Domestic workers, Fruit and flower vendors, Construction workers, Households headed by widows, disabled persons, or senior citizens (60+ years) with no social support.

    • Key Features:
  • Food grain subsidies:
    • Wheat – Rs. 2 per kg
    • Rice – Rs. 3 per kg
    • Sugar – Rs. 18.50 per kg (1 kg per family per month)
    • Each family gets 35 kg of food grains per month.
  • Special Ration Cards: Different colors for different states (e.g., Yellow Card in Kerala, Pink Card in Telangana).
  • Monthly food grain allocation: 8.51 lakh tonnes.

    • Expansion & Impact: Initially covered 1 crore families, Later expanded to 1.5 crore families (23% of BPL families), Further expanded to 3 crore families, benefiting 15 crore people, Total monthly food grain distribution increased from 62.5 lakh tonnes to 87.5 lakh tonnes, AAY budget increased by Rs. 4,000 crores to meet rising demand.

    PM POSHAN (Mid Day Meals)

    Launched: 15th August 1995
    Ministry: Ministry of Education (formerly Ministry of Human Resource Development)
    Renamed: In September 2021, it was renamed PM-POSHAN (Pradhan Mantri Poshan Shakti Nirman)
    Duration: 2021-22 to 2025-26 (Current phase)
    Budget: ₹1,30,794.90 crore
    Objectives:

  • To increase enrollment of disadvantaged children in schools.
  • To boost attendance and retention rates.
  • To provide nutritional support to school children, especially in drought-affected areas.
  • To reduce classroom hunger and support cognitive development.
  • To empower women by providing cooking-related employment.
  • To improve socialization among children by serving meals in a communal setting.

    • Target Groups: Children from Class 1 to 8 studying in: Government schools, Government-aided schools, Local body schools, Education Guarantee Scheme (EGS) & Alternative Innovative Education (AIE) centers, Madrasas & Maqtabs supported under Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan (SSA), Recently, it has been extended to pre-primary students (Bal Vatikas).
    Nutritional Norms:
  • Primary Students (Classes 1-5):
    • 450 calories
    • 12g protein
  • Upper Primary Students (Classes 6-8):
    • 700 calories
    • 20g protein
  • Micronutrients, including iron, folic acid, and vitamin A, are provided under the School Health Programme.

    • Key Features:
  • World’s largest school meal program (Covers 11.80 crore children across 11.20 lakh schools as per 2021-22).
  • Cost-sharing: Centrally Sponsored Scheme (60% Centre, 40% States).
  • First State to Implement: Tamil Nadu.
  • Public-Private Partnership (PPP): NGOs like Akshaya Patra assist in meal preparation and distribution.
  • Special Provisions: Cooking cost assistance, Transportation subsidy for food grains, Kitchen-cum-stores and kitchen devices for hygienic food preparation, Summer meal provision in drought-affected areas.
  • Monitoring & Evaluation: Accredited labs test meal quality monthly, School Management Committees (SMCs) oversee implementation, Food allowances provided if meals are not served for 3 consecutive days.

    • Recent Developments:
  • Proposal to include milk in the mid-day meal.
  • Increased community participation in monitoring and execution.
  • Digital tracking for transparency & efficiency.

    • National Food Security Mission (NFSM)

    Launched By: Government of India
    Launch Date: 2007-08
    Implemented In: 638 districts across 29 states
    Ministry: Ministry of Agriculture & Farmers Welfare
    Target Groups: Small & marginal farmers, SC/ST farmers, women farmers
    Key Objectives:

  • Increase production and productivity of rice, wheat, pulses, and coarse cereals sustainably.
  • Restore soil fertility and productivity at the individual farm level.
  • Enhance farm income to boost farmers’ confidence.

    • Implementation & Achievements:
  • During the 11th Five-Year Plan, NFSM achieved an increase of 20 million metric tonnes (MT) of food grains.
  • Continued during the 12th Five-Year Plan (2012-17) with a target of 25 million MT of additional food grain production.
  • Coarse cereals included in the mission from 2014-15.
  • Between 2017-18 and 2019-20, NFSM aimed to increase food grain production by 13 million MT.
  • In 2017-18, India achieved a record food grain production of 285.01 million MT.

    • Key Components of NFSM:
  • NFSM-Food Grain Crops (Rice, Wheat, Pulses)
  • NFSM-Coarse Cereals
  • NFSM-Commercial Crops (Sugarcane, Cotton, Jute)
  • NFSM-Oilseeds & Oil Palm
  • NFSM-Seed Village Programme

    • UNIVERSAL ACCESS TO SAFE AND NUTRITIOUS FOOD

    By 2030, end hunger and ensure access by all people, in particular the poor and people in vulnerable situations, including infants, to safe, nutritious and sufficient food all year round.

    END ALL FORMS OF MALNUTRITION

    By 2030, end all forms of malnutrition, including achieving, by 2025, the internationally agreed targets on stunting and wasting in children under 5 years of age, and address the nutritional needs of adolescent girls, pregnant and lactating women and older persons.

    DOUBLE THE PRODUCTIVITY AND INCOMES OF SMALL-SCALE FOOD PRODUCERS

    By 2030, double the agricultural productivity and incomes of small-scale food producers, in particular women, indigenous peoples, family farmers, pastoralists and fishers, including through secure and equal access to land, other productive resources and inputs, knowledge, financial services, markets and opportunities for value addition and non-farm employment.

    SUSTAINABLE FOOD PRODUCTION AND RESILIENT AGRICULTURAL PRACTICES

    By 2030, ensure sustainable food production systems and implement resilient agricultural practices that increase productivity and production, that help maintain ecosystems, that strengthen capacity for adaptation to climate change, extreme weather, drought, flooding and other disasters and that progressively improve land and soil quality.

    MAINTAIN THE GENETIC DIVERSITY IN FOOD PRODUCTION

    By 2020, maintain the genetic diversity of seeds, cultivated plants and farmed and domesticated animals and their related wild species, including through soundly managed and diversified seed and plant banks at the national, regional and international levels, and promote access to and fair and equitable sharing of benefits arising from the utilization of genetic resources and associated traditional knowledge, as internationally agreed.

    INVEST IN RURAL INFRASTRUCTURE, AGRICULTURAL RESEARCH, TECHNOLOGY AND GENE BANKS

    Increase investment, including through enhanced international cooperation, in rural infrastructure, agricultural research and extension services, technology development and plant and livestock gene banks in order to enhance agricultural productive capacity in developing countries, in particular least developed countries.

    PREVENT AGRICULTURAL TRADE RESTRICTIONS, MARKET DISTORTIONS AND EXPORT SUBSIDIES

    Correct and prevent trade restrictions and distortions in world agricultural markets, including through the parallel elimination of all forms of agricultural export subsidies and all export measures with equivalent effect, in accordance with the mandate of the Doha Development Round.

    ENSURE STABLE FOOD COMMODITY MARKETS AND TIMELY ACCESS TO INFORMATION

    Adopt measures to ensure the proper functioning of food commodity markets and their derivatives and facilitate timely access to market information, including on food reserves, in order to help limit extreme food price volatility.
